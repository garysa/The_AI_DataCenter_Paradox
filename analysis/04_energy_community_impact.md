# 04 — Energy, Water, and Community Opposition

## The Resource Demands Are Enormous

AI data centres are not merely large buildings with computers. They are industrial-scale
power and water consumers that transform the infrastructure of the regions that host them.

### Electricity

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| US data centre consumption (2023) | 176 TWh — equivalent to 16 million homes for a year |
| US data centre share of electricity use (2025) | ~5% |
| Projected US data centre share (2030) | ~12% |
| Generative AI power demand CAGR | 65% annually |
| AI inference demand growth | 122% CAGR — fastest growing segment |
| Global data centre power growth to 2030 | 165% increase projected |

### Water

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Typical medium data centre water consumption | ~110 million gallons / year |
| Equivalent to | ~1,000 households' annual usage |
| Microsoft's data centre in Agriport, Netherlands | 84 million litres of drinking water in one year |

## The Grid Is Breaking Under the Load

The electricity demands of AI data centres are outstripping regional grid capacity in many areas:

**Northern Virginia (Loudoun County — "Data Centre Alley"):**
- Home to the world's largest concentration of data centres
- Utilities warned that existing substations and high-voltage lines are approaching limits
- July 2024: a voltage fluctuation triggered simultaneous disconnection of 60 data centres,
  producing a 1,500 MW power surplus — emergency action required to prevent cascading outages
- **Electricity rates in PJM region (mid-Atlantic) increased up to 20% in summer 2025**
- **Wholesale electricity now costs up to 267% more than 5 years ago** in data-centre-heavy areas
- **Capacity charges increased 833%** for 2025–2026 in some adjacent markets
- A typical 100 MW Amazon data centre contributes $3.4M to grid costs in 2025, rising to
  $6.1M by 2030

**Texas:**
Grid already vulnerable to brownouts. San Marcos council paused a $1.5B data centre
proposal after residents raised energy security concerns.

**Grid interconnection queues now stretch 5–10 years** in many regions — even willing utilities
cannot deliver power fast enough to meet data centre demand.

---

## Community Opposition: $64 Billion Blocked

> **"$64 billion of data centre projects have been blocked or delayed amid local, bipartisan
> opposition."**
> — Data Center Watch (May 2024 – March 2025)

This is not a fringe movement. It is a rapidly growing, bipartisan political force.

### Key Cases

**Tucson, Arizona:**
City Council voted unanimously to oppose Project Blue — a proposed $3.6B Amazon data centre
campus — citing estimated water consumption and potential energy cost increases for local
ratepayers.

**San Marcos, Texas:**
Council moved to pause a $1.5B, 200-acre proposal. Residents cited data centre energy demand
in a region already vulnerable to brownouts.

**Prince George's County, Maryland:**
Leaders imposed a 180-day pause on new data centre proposals after community demonstrations
and a petition with over 20,000 signatures.

**Ireland — Dublin and Greater Dublin:**
Effective ban on new data centres until 2028 due to grid capacity concerns. Data centres
already expected to consume **one-third of the island's electricity by 2026**. Risk of
rolling blackouts.

**Netherlands:**
Amsterdam imposed effective ban. Meta paused its Zeewolde mega-campus plan amid environmental
objections. Data centres in North Holland used **84 million litres of drinking water** in
one year. Amsterdam imposed 20% water consumption cuts during 2024 water-stress alerts.
Dutch municipalities now require operators to deliver **net-positive benefits** (wetland
restoration, heat reuse) before permits are issued.

**California:**
Big Tech successfully blocked an AI data centre energy study bill, but opposition is entrenching.

**National:**
In December 2025, hundreds of environmental groups formally asked Congress to **halt building
of all new data centres**. Change.org counted **113 anti-data-centre petitions** in 2025,
totalling ~50,000 signatures — a sharp surge from prior years.

---

## The Power Bill Impact on Ordinary People

Bloomberg documented the direct link between data centre concentration and household electricity
bills: *"How AI Data Centres Are Sending Your Power Bill Soaring."*

Amazon's own press team pushed back with a study claiming data centres don't raise electricity
bills — but this was immediately countered by independent analysis showing:

- Wholesale electricity costs up 267% over 5 years in data-centre-heavy zones
- Capacity charges (the mechanism by which grid strain is distributed to all users) up 833%
  for 2025–2026

The political economy here is explosive: data centres generate profits for distant shareholders
while distributing costs (grid strain, water depletion, electricity price increases) to local
communities. This is a classic NIMBY trigger — and unlike most NIMBY issues, the grievances
are empirically grounded.

---

## The Nuclear Response

Facing opposition on grid strain and carbon, hyperscalers are increasingly moving toward nuclear:

- **AWS**: Acquired a 960 MW nuclear-powered campus; invested in SMR developer X-energy
- **Microsoft**: Revived Three Mile Island; exploring microreactors for modular deployments
- **Nuclear capacity factor**: >92.5% vs wind 35%, solar 25%, gas 56%

However, nuclear is not a short-term fix. SMRs (Small Modular Reactors) are still years from
commercial deployment at scale. Existing large-scale nuclear requires regulatory approval, grid
interconnection, and construction timelines measured in decades, not months.

---

## Is the Opposition Winning?

The political momentum is real. In 2025:
- $64B in projects blocked or delayed (US alone, 12-month window)
- Ireland and Netherlands implementing effective moratoriums
- National advocacy groups demanding construction freeze
- State legislatures in California, Texas, Virginia introducing data-centre-specific legislation

The industry is adapting: moving toward offshore locations, underserved regions with cheap
power, rural areas with excess grid capacity. But these locations have their own constraints
(fibre connectivity, skilled labour, water availability).
