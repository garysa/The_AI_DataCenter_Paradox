# 12 — The Final Thesis: Coming Down to Earth

## The Conclusion the Data Points To

After analysing 100+ sources across investment timelines, hardware cycles, AI profitability,
community opposition, bubble risk, Oracle's specific bet, and AGI geopolitics, the most
coherent conclusion is not the one the market narrative has been selling.

**The hyperscalers will not be racing to AGI. They will be quietly embedding narrow AI
into businesses they already own — and making money doing it.**

The AGI hype will follow the same arc as the dot-com hype. It will come down to earth.
And what will be left standing is not the moonshot — it is the infrastructure, the practical
applications, and the companies that had profitable businesses before AI arrived.

---

## The Dot-Com Parallel Is More Exact Than Most Admit

In 1999–2000, the narrative was:
- The internet will change everything
- First-movers win everything
- Revenue doesn't matter — growth does
- Anyone not building internet infrastructure is already behind

In 2025–2026, the narrative is:
- AI will change everything
- First-movers (AGI) win everything
- Revenue doesn't matter — compute scale does
- Anyone not building AI infrastructure is already behind

**What actually happened after the dot-com correction:**

The internet *did* change everything — but over 15–20 years, not 3.
The winners were not the pure-play internet companies (Pets.com, Webvan, Kozmo).
The winners were companies that had real businesses and integrated the internet into them:

| Pre-internet company | What they did | Outcome |
|---------------------|---------------|---------|
| Walmart | Added e-commerce to retail | Survived |
| Barnes & Noble | Added online ordering | Survived (barely) |
| Banks | Added online banking | Thrived |
| Airlines | Added online booking | Thrived |
| Amazon | *Was* the internet company | Won — but took 10 years to profit |

The pure-play internet bets either failed or took a decade of losses to prove out.

**The AI equivalent is already visible:**

| Company type | AI strategy | Likely outcome |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| AWS/Azure/GCP | Embed AI in existing profitable cloud | Survive and grow |
| SAP/Oracle ERP | Embed AI in existing enterprise software | Strong — data gravity holds |
| Healthcare/finance incumbents | Narrow AI for specific workflows | Profitable case-by-case |
| OpenAI | Pure-play AGI bet | Uncertain — needs 2029+ to prove |
| CoreWeave | Pure-play GPU cloud | High failure risk |
| Stargate/Oracle AI infra | AGI infrastructure bet | Acute financial distress now |

## What "Narrow AI in the Current Business" Actually Means

The hyperscalers don't need AGI to make billions from AI. They need:

### 1. AI-enhanced existing products
- AWS: AI-powered cost optimisation, anomaly detection, database query acceleration
- Azure: Copilot embedded in Office 365 (Word, Excel, Teams, Outlook) — sold to 400M+ users
- Google Cloud: AI search, AI ad targeting, AI translation — already generating revenue
- Oracle: AI inside Oracle Database and Fusion ERP — sold to existing customers who can't leave

**These products are already generating revenue. Today. Without AGI.**

Microsoft's GitHub Copilot has 1.8 million paid subscribers. That is not AGI. That is a
narrow code autocomplete tool. It generates real money at a known margin.

### 2. AI as infrastructure efficiency
Every hyperscaler is using AI internally to:
- Optimise data centre cooling (Google DeepMind cut cooling energy 40%)
- Predict hardware failures before they happen
- Optimise routing, storage tiering, and network traffic
- Reduce human support costs through AI-assisted triage

These internal efficiency gains directly improve margins without needing to sell a single
AI product externally.

### 3. Vertical AI for specific industries
The highest-ROI AI deployments are narrow, specific, and boring:
- AI for radiology (detecting tumours in scans) — FDA approved, reimbursable
- AI for fraud detection in payments — saves billions annually, proven ROI
- AI for predictive maintenance in manufacturing — measurable uptime improvement
- AI for contract review in law firms — billable hours saved, clear cost reduction
- AI for crop yield prediction in agriculture — measurable output improvement

None of these require AGI. None require H100 clusters. Many run on commodity hardware.
All of them have clear, quantifiable ROI that business decision-makers can approve.

## The Slowdown Signal Is Already in the Data

The hyperscalers are not publicly admitting slowdown — they are still announcing enormous
capex numbers to signal competitive commitment. But the underlying signals tell a different story:

**GPU rental price collapse:**
H100 rates fell 64% in 2025. If demand were insatiable, prices would hold. They didn't.
The market is telling us compute supply is outrunning committed demand.

**Utilisation rates not disclosed:**
No hyperscaler is publicly disclosing AI data centre utilisation rates. This is notable.
When utilisation is high, companies brag about it. Silence implies the numbers are uncomfortable.

**The DeepSeek lesson:**
When DeepSeek demonstrated that a fraction of the compute could achieve comparable results,
hyperscaler stocks fell sharply. The market interpreted this correctly: **the marginal value
of additional compute is falling faster than anyone admitted.**

**Enterprise AI adoption is slower than projected:**
Only 25% of enterprise AI initiatives have delivered expected ROI.
Fewer than 20% have been scaled enterprise-wide.
The Fortune 500 is not deploying AI at the speed the infrastructure build assumed.

**Community opposition is real and growing:**
$64 billion in projects blocked in 12 months. This is not a fringe concern —
it is a structural constraint on where and how fast data centres can be built.

## The Case-by-Case Build: What Winning Actually Looks Like

The companies that will profit from AI over the next 5–10 years are those that:

**1. Start with a problem, not a technology**
Not: "We have H100 clusters, what can we sell?"
But: "We have a fraud detection problem that costs us $2B/year — can AI solve it?"

**2. Measure ROI before scaling**
Pilot → prove ROI → scale. Not: build at hyperscale and assume demand follows.

**3. Use narrow models for narrow problems**
A fine-tuned 7B parameter model running on a single GPU server, solving one specific
problem, beats a GPT-4-class model burning $10/day in API costs for the same task.

**4. Keep data where it lives (the Oracle insight that is actually correct)**
Moving data to the cloud to train AI models creates cost, latency, security risk, and
regulatory exposure. The companies that embed AI at the data source — inside the database,
inside the ERP, inside the production system — win the enterprise AI market.

**5. Don't wait for AGI**
The companies building profitable AI businesses today are not waiting for artificial
general intelligence. They are deploying narrow, specific, measurable tools — and banking
the returns. By the time AGI arrives (if it does, in any timeline relevant to today's
investment), these companies will have 5–10 years of compounded narrow AI advantage.

## What Happens to the AGI Bets

**OpenAI:** Most likely outcome is not failure but acquisition or transformation.
Microsoft's $13B investment creates a strong incentive to ensure OpenAI survives.
OpenAI becomes a product (integrated into Microsoft 365, Azure, Windows) rather than a
standalone AGI lab. The AGI mission gets subordinated to the commercial reality.
Altman remains as figurehead; the company becomes Microsoft Research with a consumer brand.

**Oracle/Stargate:** Most likely outcome is a scaled-back version of Stargate — the
Abilene cluster completes, the additional 5 sites are delayed or partially cancelled.
Oracle sells Cerner, cuts 20–30K staff, stabilises debt at $100–110B, and pivots its
narrative from "AI data centre builder" back to "enterprise AI platform." The OCI business
continues growing, but at 30–40% rather than 68%. Ellison keeps his 41% stake and waits.

**Pure-play GPU cloud (CoreWeave, Lambda, etc.):** Consolidation. The weakest operators
fail or merge. The survivors are absorbed into hyperscalers or find niche markets
(scientific computing, film/VFX rendering, specific enterprise verticals). The GPU-rental
commodity market stabilises at lower margins with fewer players.

**Nvidia:** Continues to win. Regardless of whether AGI or narrow AI dominates, GPUs are
the hardware of the current generation. Nvidia's moat is real, its margins are real
($29B FCF in FY2025), and it benefits from every AI application regardless of AGI timeline.
The risk to Nvidia is custom silicon (AWS Trainium, Google TPUs) gradually reducing
hyperscaler Nvidia dependency — but this plays out over 5–10 years, not immediately.

## The 5–10 Year Realistic AI Landscape

By 2030–2035, the AI industry will look like this:

**What will be true:**
- Every major enterprise software product has AI embedded (like spreadsheets have formulas)
- Narrow AI is generating measurable, quantified value in healthcare, finance, law, manufacturing
- The hyperscalers have profitable AI businesses — not from AGI, from AI-enhanced cloud services
- GPU compute has become a commodity, with prices reflecting supply/demand balance
- Several AI infrastructure companies (not named AWS/Azure/GCP) have failed or been absorbed
- Community/regulatory frameworks for data centres have matured — energy and water limits exist

**What will probably not be true:**
- AGI (as defined by recursive self-improvement beyond human capability) widely deployed
- AI replacing the majority of knowledge work (it will augment it)
- Data centres operating at the utilisation rates needed to justify 2025–2026 capex levels
- Oracle as a dominant standalone AI infrastructure company (it will be an enterprise AI platform)
- The current wave of pure-play AI companies still independent (most will merge or fail)

**What is genuinely uncertain:**
- Whether OpenAI proves out the consumer AI market at scale (ChatGPT is real; $200B revenue by 2030 is speculative)
- Whether AGI timelines compress dramatically (breakthrough research could change everything)
- Whether China or the US "wins" the AI race in any meaningful sense
- Whether the compute efficiency revolution (DeepSeek-style) continues to compress the hardware requirement

## The Honest Summary

The dot-com era taught us that transformative technology is real, the hype is always excessive,
the infrastructure survives, and the profitable businesses are the ones that used the technology
to solve real problems for real customers — not the ones that bet the company on the technology
itself being the product.

**AI will be transformative. Over 10–20 years. Case by case. Problem by problem.**

The hyperscalers know this. They are building the infrastructure while publicly maintaining the
AGI narrative — because the narrative attracts capital, talent, and customers. But their actual
product strategies (Copilot in Office, AI in databases, AI-optimised cloud services) are
deeply, deliberately narrow.

Larry Ellison knows this too. His 47 years in enterprise software taught him that databases
won not by being the most exciting technology but by being the most embedded one. His Oracle
AI strategy is ultimately the same bet: embed AI so deeply into the enterprise that customers
cannot leave.

The tragedy of Stargate — if it fails — is that the right insight (AI infrastructure matters
for national security and enterprise competitiveness) was pursued with the wrong capital
structure (too much debt, too fast, against a single customer's unproven revenue).

**The insight survives even if the bet doesn't.**

And 10 years from now, when AI is embedded in every hospital, every bank, every factory floor,
and every legal department — not as AGI, but as dozens of narrow tools solving specific problems
at measurable ROI — the people who will have been right are the ones who said:

> *Come down to earth. See what we've got. Work up, case by case.*
