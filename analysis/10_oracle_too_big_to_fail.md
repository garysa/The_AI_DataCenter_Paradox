# 10 — Is Oracle Becoming the First Tech Company "Too Big to Fail"?

## The Situation as of February 2026: The Banks Are Already Leaving

The hypothesis is no longer theoretical. As of February 2026:

- **US banks have already retreated** from Oracle's data centre project financing
- **Oracle may cut 30,000 jobs** — its biggest layoff in company history — to free up cash
- **OpenAI has shifted near-term compute needs** away from Oracle to Microsoft and Amazon
- **Oracle delays multiple OpenAI data centres** by one year or more (2027 → 2028)
- **Oracle raised $25 billion in bonds** in a single deal — one of the largest in corporate history
- **Lenders doubled Oracle's borrowing rate** since September 2025, pricing it like junk

The question "when will banks bail?" has a partial answer: **they already have, in the project
financing market.** The question now is whether the bond market, equity investors, and
ultimately the US government reach the same conclusion.

---

## The "Too Big to Fail" Test: What Makes a Company Systemic?

For a company to qualify as "too big to fail" (TBTF) in the sense that matters — where
government intervention is considered to prevent collapse — several conditions must be met:

| Criterion | Oracle's Status |
|-----------|----------------|
| Scale of debt enmeshed in financial system | ✅ $130B+ debt; $100B ABS/bonds held by institutions |
| Critical infrastructure role | ✅ Stargate = US strategic AI infrastructure |
| Systemic counterparty exposure | ✅ OpenAI, SoftBank, Microsoft chains |
| National security framing | ✅ Explicitly — Stargate is national security AI |
| Regulatory capture / government relationship | ✅ Presidential announcement; emergency energy permits |
| Too many jobs to let fail | ⚠️ 140,000 employees; cutting 30,000 already |
| No immediate successor | ⚠️ AWS/Azure/Google could absorb — eventually |

Oracle meets most of the structural criteria. But the government has explicitly **denied** that
a bailout is coming.

## Breaking: The Banks Have Already Pulled Back

### The TD Cowen Report (February 2026)

Investment bank TD Cowen issued a report in early February 2026 that triggered a further
sell-off in Oracle shares:

**Key findings:**
- Oracle is considering cutting **20,000 to 30,000 jobs** and selling business units
  to free up $8–10 billion in cash flow
- US banks have **pulled back from data centre project financing** for Oracle
- Lenders have roughly **doubled the interest rate premiums** charged to Oracle since September 2025
- Oracle may need to sell its **Cerner healthcare software unit** (acquired for $28.3B in 2022)
  to raise capital
- TD Cowen estimates Oracle faces **$156 billion in required capital expenditure** commitments

### OpenAI's Demand Shift — The Most Dangerous Signal

The most damaging data point in the February 2026 environment:

> **"OpenAI has shifted its near-term capacity needs to Microsoft and Amazon."**
> — TD Cowen / CIO Magazine, February 2026

This is not Oracle losing a bid. This is Oracle's primary customer — the source of $300B of
its $523B backlog — redirecting compute spending elsewhere. If this trend continues:

- Oracle builds massive data centre capacity for OpenAI demand that is now partially going elsewhere
- Oracle carries the capex and debt regardless
- The $60B/yr revenue assumption from OpenAI contracts looks increasingly optimistic
- Oracle's delivery delays (2027 → 2028) may have triggered OpenAI to seek alternative supply

### Asian Banks Stepping In — At What Cost?

Where US banks retreat, Asian banks have stepped in — but at a **premium rate**. This is not
rescue financing; it is opportunistic lending at terms that reflect the elevated risk. Oracle's
weighted average cost of debt is rising precisely when its free cash flow is most negative.

## The Systemic Contagion Chain

Oracle's failure — a genuine default or forced restructuring — would not be contained to Oracle
shareholders. The contagion chain looks like this:

```
Oracle fails to service debt
        ↓
$25B+ bond holders (pension funds, insurers, SWFs) take losses
        ↓
ABS/structured products backed by Oracle data centre leases impaired
        ↓
OpenAI loses primary infrastructure provider mid-build
        ↓
Stargate project disrupted — US national security AI capability delayed
        ↓
SoftBank (Stargate co-partner) faces write-downs on AI commitments
        ↓
Nvidia revenue declines (Oracle was one of largest GB200 buyers)
        ↓
AI infrastructure confidence crisis — broad repricing of sector
        ↓
Hyperscaler capex cuts — cascade into data centre developers, REIT sector
```

This is not a dotcom collapse where a few overvalued startups disappear. This is the potential
disruption of infrastructure that the US government has explicitly designated as **strategic
national security capability**.

---

## What Happens at Each Failure Stage?

### Stage 1: "Soft Failure" — Job Cuts + Asset Sales (NOW)
**Already happening.** Oracle cuts 30,000 staff ($8–10B cash release), sells Cerner ($15–25B),
uses proceeds to service debt. Stock recovers partially. The AI build slows.

**Who gets hurt?** 30,000 Oracle employees. Cerner healthcare software customers. Oracle's
enterprise software customers who are now uncertain about long-term product support.

**Government response required?** No. This is a corporate restructuring, not a systemic failure.

### Stage 2: "Project Financing Failure" — Oracle Abandons Specific Data Centres
Oracle completes the Abilene/Texas cluster (already mostly built) but abandons planned
expansion in New Mexico, Ohio, Wisconsin sites. Stargate's 7 GW plan contracts to 3–4 GW.

**Who gets hurt?** Communities promised jobs and economic development. Construction
contractors. Local energy utilities that have committed grid capacity to planned facilities.

**Government response required?** Political embarrassment — Trump's flagship AI project
visibly downsizes. Emergency energy permit commitments become stranded government actions.

### Stage 3: "Debt Restructuring" — Formal Credit Event
Oracle negotiates with bondholders to extend maturities, accept haircuts, or convert debt
to equity. Triggers forced selling by investment-grade-only bond mandates. CDS settlements.
Ellison's personal stake diluted by restructuring. Stock drops to $80–100 range.

**Who gets hurt?** Bondholders (pension funds, insurers). Shareholders. Oracle enterprise
customers face contract uncertainty and potential service disruption during reorganisation.

**Government response required?** Possibly. If the debt restructuring threatens to cascade
into broader credit markets, the Fed may need to provide liquidity. FDIC/banking regulators
may need to manage exposure at US bank counterparties.

### Stage 4: "Full Failure" — Chapter 11 Bankruptcy
Oracle enters formal bankruptcy protection. Data centres in mid-construction are halted.
OpenAI's compute pipeline is severed. Stargate collapses.

**Government response required?** Almost certainly yes — not a financial bailout in the
TARP sense, but emergency government acquisition or nationalisation of Stargate infrastructure
to preserve national security AI capability. The Abilene cluster alone (450,000 GB200 GPUs)
represents irreplaceable national security compute capacity that no private buyer could absorb
immediately.

---

## The Government's Stated Position vs. The Revealed Position

**Stated:** AI Czar David Sacks: *"There will be no federal bailout for AI. If one fails,
others will take its place."*

**Revealed:** The US government:
- Used emergency declarations to expedite Stargate energy permits
- Framed Stargate explicitly as national security infrastructure
- Has export-controlled AI chips to maintain US AI supremacy
- Is funding the NSF, DARPA, and DOE with billions in AI research
- Has embedded AI infrastructure in the National Security Council's strategic priorities

The gap between "no bailout" rhetoric and "strategic infrastructure" framing is where the
actual policy lives. A formal Oracle bankruptcy that threatens Stargate would face enormous
pressure from the national security establishment to find a solution — whether through
emergency government financing, forced acquisition, or arranged merger.

The **AI Now Institute** directly addressed this in a research paper titled:
**"Too Big to Fail: Infrastructure and Capital Push"** — noting that the concentration of AI
infrastructure in a handful of companies creates systemic dependencies that governments may
ultimately feel compelled to support.

## The Cerner Sale: The Most Revealing Signal

Oracle paid **$28.3 billion** for Cerner (electronic health records software) in 2022 —
its largest-ever acquisition. Now analysts say Oracle is considering selling it.

If Oracle sells Cerner, it signals:
1. The company's financial situation is more urgent than public statements suggest
2. The AI infrastructure bet has completely displaced the healthcare strategy
3. Ellison is willing to dismantle the corporate diversification he built to fund the Stargate bet

A Cerner sale would likely raise $15–25 billion — meaningful against $50B/yr capex but not
transformative. It also signals to the bond market that Oracle is in asset-sale mode, which
historically triggers accelerating debt concerns, not relief.

## The Comparison That Terrifies Credit Analysts: Telecom 2001

In 2001, WorldCom — the second-largest US long-distance telephone company — collapsed in an
$11B accounting fraud after wildly over-investing in fibre-optic infrastructure during the
dotcom boom. The assets were real (fibre in the ground), but the revenue model was fictional.

WorldCom filed the **largest corporate bankruptcy in US history** at that time.
The fibre survived. WorldCom did not.

Oracle is not committing fraud. Its data centres are real, its backlog is (mostly) real,
and its enterprise software moat is real. But the structural parallel is uncomfortable:
- Genuine infrastructure being built
- Based on revenue projections that may not materialise at the contracted scale
- Financed with debt that must be serviced before the revenue arrives
- With a single concentrated customer (OpenAI as WorldCom's wholesale bandwidth buyer)

## The Timeline to "Too Big to Fail" Moment

Based on the data available as of February 2026:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **Nov 2025** | Barclays: cash out by Nov 2026 without action |
| **Dec 2025** | Oracle bonds trade at junk spreads; death cross forms |
| **Jan 2026** | Oracle raises $25B in bonds (one of largest ever) |
| **Feb 2026** | US banks retreat; Oracle eyes 30K layoffs; TD Cowen alarm |
| **Feb 2026** | OpenAI shifts near-term compute to Microsoft/Amazon |
| **Mar 2026** | **Q3 FY2026 earnings — critical inflection point** |
| **Jun 2026** | **Q4 FY2026 — must show FCF improvement or rating cut** |
| **Nov 2026** | Barclays' cash depletion window |
| **2027** | First OpenAI $60B/yr payments due under $300B contract |
| **2027–2028** | Oracle data centre deliveries rescheduled |

**The "too big to fail" moment — if it comes — is most likely between Q4 FY2026 (June 2026)
and the formal start of the OpenAI contract payments (2027).**

If Oracle reaches that window with negative free cash flow, junk-level borrowing costs,
and delayed revenue, the choice becomes: structured government intervention or unstructured
bankruptcy. Given the national security framing of Stargate, the US government would be
under enormous pressure to choose intervention.

## What Oracle Is Actually Betting On

Larry Ellison's rational calculation, stripped of the hype:

1. The $300B OpenAI contract begins generating serious revenue in 2027
2. That revenue, at 30–40% margins, generates $18–24B/yr in operating profit from OCI alone
3. The enterprise database moat plus OCI cloud revenue together reach $50B/yr FCF by 2028
4. That FCF services the debt, funds continued hardware refresh, and makes Oracle the
   dominant AI infrastructure company of the late 2020s

This requires:
- OpenAI surviving and paying
- Oracle completing its data centres on time
- No rate cuts to its borrowing costs getting worse
- No major hyperscaler technology leapfrog

Every one of these conditions is currently under stress. The banks have noticed.
The question is whether Oracle — and Ellison — can hold the nerve long enough for
the revenue to arrive.
