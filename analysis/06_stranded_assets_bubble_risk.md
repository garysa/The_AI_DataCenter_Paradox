# 06 — Stranded Assets and the Bubble Risk

## Are We Watching a Dot-Com Replay?

The parallel is hard to ignore. In the late 1990s:
- Vast quantities of fibre-optic cable were laid based on projections of exponential internet
  demand growth
- Companies took on enormous debt to fund infrastructure buildout
- Many of these companies went bankrupt in 2001–2003
- The fibre itself survived and became the backbone of the internet we use today — but most
  investors who financed it lost everything

**In 2025:**
- Vast quantities of GPU clusters are being assembled based on projections of exponential
  AI demand growth
- Companies are taking on enormous debt to fund infrastructure buildout
- Many of these companies (particularly pure-play AI cloud providers) may face financial
  distress as GPU prices collapse and revenue trails CapEx

The infrastructure may ultimately prove useful. But that doesn't mean the investors survive.

---

## Warning Signs: The Numbers That Should Worry Investors

### The debt surge
- Top 5 hyperscalers raised a **record $108B in debt in 2025** (3× the 9-year average)
- Debt financing for AI data centres surged to **$125B in 2025** (from $15B in 2024)
- AI data centre ABS market: **>$100B** in packaged bonds sold to institutional investors

### The CoreWeave canary
CoreWeave's March 2025 IPO is the most cited warning sign:
- Backed by Blackstone and Magnetar
- Accumulated **$12.9B in debt in just two years**
- Forced to slash valuation from **$35B to $23B** before IPO
- Revenue dependent on a narrow base of customers (primarily Microsoft)

### The non-renewal risk materialises
In December 2025, **Fermi Inc.** disclosed that an investment-grade tenant had terminated a
$150M agreement — demonstrating that non-renewal risk previously described as theoretical is
now happening in practice.

### Amazon investor reaction
Amazon's stock dropped **~8–10%** on announcement of its $200B capex plan for 2026 —
reflecting investor nervousness about payback timelines, despite Amazon being the most
financially robust player in the space.

## The Stranded Asset Anatomy

### Hardware obsolescence
- A data centre optimised for 2023-era H100 GPUs **may be functionally obsolete by 2028**
- Retrofitting for next-generation liquid-cooled hardware costs 7–10% more than building new
- Many existing air-cooled facilities **cannot physically accommodate** B200/GB200-era densities

### Lease vs. hardware mismatch
| Element | Typical Lifespan |
|---------|-----------------|
| Building/civil works | 30 years |
| Power infrastructure | 20–30 years |
| Data centre lease terms | 10–15 years |
| GPU competitive frontier life | **18–24 months** |

The GPU that justifies the 10-year lease is obsolete before the lease is half-expired.

### The ABS time bomb
Data centre Asset-Backed Securities package lease payments as bonds sold to pension funds,
insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds. If:
- H100-era demand collapses post-Blackwell deployment
- Residual value of 2023–2025 vintage infrastructure approaches zero
- Lessees (CoreWeave, etc.) fail to renew or go bankrupt

Then the **equity tranches of ABS structures could be wiped out** — similar to how
mortgage-backed securities functioned in 2007–2008.

---

## The Bull Case: Why This Might Not Be a Bubble

### Hyperscalers have real profitable businesses
Unlike dot-com companies, today's infrastructure financiers (AWS, Google, Microsoft) generate
enormous free cash flow from existing operations:
- Nvidia: $29B free cash flow in fiscal 2025
- Microsoft Azure AI revenue: >$10B/quarter

These companies can sustain infrastructure spending through cycles in ways that pure dot-com
plays could not.

### Infrastructure tends to survive its builders
The fibre laid in 1999 powers the internet of 2025. Even if individual data centre companies
fail, the physical infrastructure (buildings, power connections, cooling systems) retains value
and gets acquired at distressed prices. New buyers inherit lower cost bases.

### Long-term contracts provide some cushion
Data centre leases of 10–15 years from investment-grade tenants (Microsoft, Google, Amazon)
provide a cash flow floor that pure dot-com companies lacked.

### Demand forecasts remain robust
AI inference demand is growing at 122% CAGR. Even with efficiency gains from DeepSeek-style
algorithmic improvements, the addressable market for compute is expanding rapidly (new use
cases: robotics, autonomous vehicles, AI agents, drug discovery).

---

## The "Inflection Bubble" Assessment

A late-2025 analysis concluded:
> *"The AI sector in late 2025 exhibits the classic hallmarks of an 'Inflection Bubble' —
> a period where genuine technological transformation creates real value, but speculative
> investment far exceeds what the near-term market can sustain."*

The key distinction from a pure bubble: **some of the investment will prove justified**.
The problem is not that AI data centres will never be needed — it's that too many have been
built too fast, with capital structures that cannot survive the time between investment and
full utilisation.

Yale Insights put it directly:
> *"This is how the AI bubble bursts: not with a sudden crash but with a slow-motion squeeze
> on companies that can't generate revenue fast enough to service their debt."*
