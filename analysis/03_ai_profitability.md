# 03 — AI Profitability: Are the Tenants Actually Making Money?

## The Fundamental Dependency Chain

Data centres make money by selling compute capacity. That capacity is consumed by:
1. Hyperscalers running cloud AI services (Microsoft Azure AI, Google Cloud AI, AWS Bedrock)
2. AI software companies (OpenAI, Anthropic, Cohere, Mistral, etc.)
3. Enterprises building on top of AI APIs

If the tenants aren't profitable — or worse, if they stop growing — the demand assumption
underpinning the data centre investment collapses.

---

## Hyperscalers: Real Revenue, Real Margins — But Is It AI?

The hyperscalers are generating enormous cloud revenue. The question is how much is genuinely
*AI-attributable* vs. traditional cloud services that have always been profitable:

**AWS**: Projected to surpass $100B annual revenue in 2025. Operating margins robust.
**Azure**: AI revenue exceeds $10B/quarter.
**Google Cloud**: Strong growth driven by AI workloads.

**Generative AI-specific cloud consumption more than doubled in 2024.**

However, critics note that cloud revenue is not purely AI revenue. The hyperscalers have profitable
legacy cloud businesses (EC2, blob storage, databases, SaaS) that have cross-subsidised AI
investments for years. The AI-specific ROI remains difficult to isolate.

**Only ~25% of AI initiatives have delivered expected ROI to date.**
**Fewer than 20% have been scaled enterprise-wide.**

## AI Software Companies: The Burn Rate Reality

### OpenAI

| Metric | 2025 Figures |
|--------|-------------|
| Revenue | ~$13B (run rate) |
| Cash burn | ~$9B |
| Burn as % of revenue | ~70% |
| Projected break-even | 2029–2030 |
| Projected 2030 revenue | ~$200B |

OpenAI's losses are projected to **balloon to roughly 75% of revenue by 2028** — driven
primarily by escalating compute costs. The company is betting on explosive revenue growth that
outpaces its cost structure.

Primary cost driver: **compute** — the very thing data centres sell. OpenAI is essentially
a pass-through for GPU costs, which means its profitability is directly constrained by
how efficiently it can run models and how much it can charge for API access.

### Anthropic

| Metric | 2025 Figures |
|--------|-------------|
| Revenue run rate | ~$7B (approaching) |
| Cash burn | ~$3B |
| Burn as % of revenue | ~70% |
| Projected break-even | 2027–2028 |
| 2026 revenue target | $26B |
| 2028 revenue target | $70B |

Anthropic's path looks more sustainable:
- Revenue costs growing more in line with revenue growth
- 80% of revenue from enterprise B2B contracts (stable, predictable)
- Deliberately avoiding compute-heavy image/video generation (OpenAI's costly territory)
- Projects dropping burn to ~9% of revenue by 2027

**Anthropic is expected to reach break-even before OpenAI.**

---

## The Compute Cost Trap

Both companies face a version of the same structural problem: **the more popular their models
become, the more compute they consume, the more their costs rise.**

For OpenAI in particular, each new capability (GPT-4o vision, DALL-E, Sora video generation)
adds enormously to compute requirements without necessarily adding proportional revenue.

This creates a paradox: success requires scale, but scale requires compute, and compute costs
are the primary barrier to profitability.

---

## The $600B Revenue Gap (Sequoia's Analysis)

Sequoia Capital's David Cahn calculated the minimum revenue the AI industry must generate
annually just to justify the hardware being purchased:

**Formula:**
> Nvidia GPU revenue forecast
> × 2 (full data centre cost multiplier)
> × 2 (50% gross margin assumption for end customers)
> = **~$600B required annual revenue**

**Current reality**: ~$25B generated by AI services in 2025.

Even a generous estimate of all major tech companies generating $10B each from AI services
still leaves a **$500B+ gap**.

As of December 2025, Cahn updated his analysis: **the gap has widened, not narrowed.**

---

## What Happens If the Revenue Doesn't Materialise?

**Scenario 1 — Demand growth catches up:**
AI adoption accelerates, enterprises scale usage, new killer applications emerge (robotics,
autonomous vehicles, AI agents, drug discovery), and revenue grows to meet infrastructure costs
over a 5–10 year horizon. The infrastructure built now becomes the backbone of a new economy.

**Scenario 2 — Efficiency overtakes demand:**
Models like DeepSeek make AI radically cheaper. The same tasks require far less compute.
Existing data centres are massively oversized. A glut of underutilised compute capacity
forces prices down, compressing margins across the board.

**Scenario 3 — Consolidation:**
Weaker AI companies fail. Compute demand concentrates in fewer hands. The hyperscalers
survive; the pure-play AI companies either become profitable or get acquired. The speculative
fringe collapses, as happened with dot-coms in 2001–2003.

**Scenario 4 — Regulatory intervention:**
Governments impose compute caps, energy limits, or carbon taxes on AI data centres. Demand
is constrained not by market forces but by policy. The infrastructure built under one
regulatory regime becomes stranded under another.
